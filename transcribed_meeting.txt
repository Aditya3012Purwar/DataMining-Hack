## Core Goal

The system wants to **predict future purchases of a customer** so the company can:

* estimate **future shopping carts**
* calculate **savings or discounts**
* recommend or automatically prepare orders

This is essentially a **demand prediction / recommendation problem**.

---

# Main Challenges

## 1. Predict what the customer will buy

To estimate savings or prepare orders, the system must predict:

* **what products the customer will purchase**
* **how often**
* **how many**

Without this prediction, the system cannot know:

* what items will be in the cart
* how much money could be saved.

However, purchase behavior is difficult because:

* Some products are bought **rarely but in large quantities** (e.g., once per year).
* Some products are bought **frequently but in small amounts**.

So simple metrics like “number of purchases” are not always enough.

---

# Product Abstraction Problem

Customers don't always need **one exact product**.

Example:

A customer wants a **notebook (laptop)**.

But they might accept:

* different suppliers
* different models
* different brands

So demand should not be predicted only at **product level**, but at **different abstraction levels**.

This leads to **hierarchical product representation**.

Example hierarchy:

```
Electronics
   └── Notebooks
         └── Lenovo
               └── Specific Model
```

This hierarchy can be built with **hierarchical clustering or taxonomy**.

# Project Milestones (Prediction Levels)

The lecturer describes **three prediction levels**.

---

## Milestone 1 — Category Prediction

Predict **how often a customer buys from a category**.

Example:

> Customer A will buy **10 products from the "Notebooks" category next year.**

Prediction target:

* **Product category (E-class)**
* **Purchase frequency**

This is the **simplest prediction level**.

---

## Milestone 2 — Category + Manufacturer

Add another feature:

* **Manufacturer / brand**

Example prediction:

> Customer A will buy **5 Lenovo notebooks next year.**

Now prediction includes:

* Category (notebook)
* Manufacturer (Lenovo)

This gives a **more realistic demand representation**.

---

## Milestone 3 — Feature-Based Product Description (Hardest)

Instead of predicting specific products, predict **product requirements**.

Example features:

* CPU: Ryzen 7
* Screen size: 15"
* Weight: 1.3 kg
* USB ports: 4

Then the system finds **products that satisfy these features**.

This approach is more realistic in **B2B procurement**, because companies specify **requirements**, not exact items.

Example:

> Milk with these requirements:

* low-fat
* long-life (UHT)
* paper packaging

Then the system searches suppliers that meet the specs.

---

# Cold Start Problem

Another challenge mentioned:

Some customers have **no historical purchase data**.

Solution:

Use **similar customers**.

Similarity can be based on:

* industry classification (e.g., **NACE code / industry taxonomy**)
* company size
* company type

Idea:

> Customers from the same industry tend to buy similar products.

Example:

```
Healthcare companies → medical supplies
Chemical companies → lab equipment
```
